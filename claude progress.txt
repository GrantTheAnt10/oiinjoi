## Slide 2

Paleontology

Paleontology studies fossils — the preserved remains of ancient life. Fossils show how living things changed over millions of years.

Examples:  Archaeopteryx  ·  Tiktaalik  ·  Ambulocetus

## Slide 3

Paleontology – Archaeopteryx

[ Image: Archaeopteryx ]

Source: Britannica – Archaeopteryx (britannica.com)

Description

• Lived about 150 million years ago
• Had feathers AND bone wings like a bird, but also had teeth and clawed fingers like a dinosaur
• Found in limestone rocks in Germany

How does this support evolution?

Archaeopteryx shows features of both dinosaurs and modern birds in the same animal. This supports evolution because it is a 'transition fossil' — evidence that birds slowly evolved from small feathered dinosaurs over a long time.

## Slide 4

Paleontology – Tiktaalik

[ Image: Tiktaalik ]

Source: National Geographic – Tiktaalik (nationalgeographic.com)

Description

• Lived about 375 million years ago in shallow water
• Had fins, but the fins had bones that looked like an arm and wrist
• Also had a flat head and a neck — features fish do not have

How does this support evolution?

Tiktaalik shows a step between fish that swim and land animals that walk. Its fin-arms support the idea that land animals evolved from fish that slowly developed limbs.

## Slide 5

Paleontology – Ambulocetus

[ Image: Ambulocetus ]

Source: Khan Academy – Evidence of Evolution (khanacademy.org)

Description

• Lived about 48 million years ago
• Had four legs but could also swim by moving its spine up and down
• Related to early ancestors of modern whales

How does this support evolution?

Ambulocetus is a 'walking whale' fossil that shows whales once lived on land. This supports evolution by showing that today's ocean-living whales came from four-legged land animals over millions of years.

## Slide 6

Biogeography

Biogeography studies where animals and plants live around the world. The pattern of where species are found helps show how they evolved in different places.

Examples:  Darwin's Finches  ·  Marsupials in Australia  ·  Cichlid Fish in African Lakes

## Slide 7

Biogeography – Darwin's Finches

[ Image: Darwin's Finches ]

Source: Khan Academy – Darwin's Finches (khanacademy.org)

Description

• Found on the Galápagos Islands in the Pacific Ocean
• 14 different species of finch, each with a different beak shape
• Each beak shape is matched to the food on that island (seeds, insects, cactus)

How does this support evolution?

All 14 finch species came from a single finch ancestor that arrived on the islands long ago. Over time, different groups changed to fit their local food source — this is a clear example of evolution through natural selection.

## Slide 8

Biogeography – Marsupials in Australia

[ Image: Marsupials in Australia ]

Source: Britannica – Marsupial (britannica.com)

Description

• Marsupials (like kangaroos and koalas) are mostly found in Australia
• Australia separated from other continents about 45 million years ago
• Marsupials evolved on their own, isolated from placental mammals elsewhere

How does this support evolution?

Because Australia was cut off from the rest of the world, its animals evolved separately. The huge variety of marsupials found only there supports the idea that isolation leads to unique evolutionary paths.

## Slide 9

Biogeography – Cichlid Fish in African Lakes

[ Image: Cichlid Fish in African Lakes ]

Source: National Geographic – Cichlid Evolution (nationalgeographic.com)

Description

• Over 500 species of cichlid fish live in Africa's Great Lakes
• Each species evolved to eat a different type of food in the lake
• All species came from just a few common ancestors

How does this support evolution?

Cichlid fish are a great example of rapid evolution in one location. Because they were isolated in separate lakes, small groups evolved into hundreds of different species — each one shaped by its environment.

## Slide 10

Embryology

Embryology is the study of how embryos (early-stage babies) develop. Related animals often look very similar as embryos, showing their shared past.

Examples:  Pharyngeal Pouches  ·  Tail in Human Embryos  ·  Limb Bud Development

## Slide 11

Embryology – Pharyngeal Pouches

[ Image: Pharyngeal Pouches ]

Source: Khan Academy – Embryology and Evolution (khanacademy.org)

Description

• Fish, frogs, chickens, pigs, and humans all have pharyngeal pouches as embryos
• In fish, these pouches become gills
• In humans, they become parts of the ear and throat

How does this support evolution?

The fact that very different animals all start with the same structures in their embryos shows that they share a common ancestor. Over time, evolution shaped those same starting structures into different body parts.

## Slide 12

Embryology – Tail in Human Embryos

[ Image: Tail in Human Embryos ]

Source: Britannica – Human Embryo Development (britannica.com)

Description

• Human embryos grow a small tail between weeks 4 and 8 of development
• The tail is then absorbed back into the body before birth
• This tail structure matches the tails seen in other vertebrate embryos

How does this support evolution?

The temporary tail in human embryos shows our connection to other animals that have tails. It is a sign left over from ancestors we shared with other mammals millions of years ago.

## Slide 13

Embryology – Limb Bud Development

[ Image: Limb Bud Development ]

Source: NCBI / National Institutes of Health – Limb Development (ncbi.nlm.nih.gov)

Description

• All vertebrate embryos (frogs, birds, mice, humans) grow limb buds in the same basic way
• The same genes control limb growth in all these animals
• The limb buds develop into arms, wings, flippers, or legs depending on the species

How does this support evolution?

Because the same genes and the same early stages are used to grow limbs in very different animals, they must have come from a shared ancestor. Evolution changed how those limbs ended up — not how they started.

## Slide 14

Anatomy

Anatomy is the study of body structures. Comparing body parts across different animals reveals shared ancestry and how species have changed over time.

Examples:  Homologous Structures  ·  Vestigial Structures – Human Tailbone  ·  Analogous Structures – Wings

## Slide 15

Anatomy – Homologous Structures

[ Image: Homologous Structures ]

Source: Khan Academy – Homologous Structures (khanacademy.org)

Description

• Humans, bats, whales, and cats all have the same set of arm/hand bones
• The bones are used for very different things — grabbing, flying, swimming, walking
• The bones look different on the outside but have the same inner structure

How does this support evolution?

Having the same bones arranged in the same way shows these animals evolved from a common ancestor. Evolution changed the size and shape of those bones to fit each animal's lifestyle.

## Slide 16

Anatomy – Vestigial Structures – Human Tailbone

[ Image: Vestigial Structures – Human Tailbone ]

Source: Britannica – Vestigial Organ (britannica.com)

Description

• Humans have a small bone at the bottom of the spine called the coccyx (tailbone)
• It does not support a tail and has very little function in people
• Other primates and many mammals use a similar structure to support their tails

How does this support evolution?

The tailbone is a vestigial structure — a leftover body part from an ancestor that actually used it. This supports evolution by showing that humans are related to animals that once had tails.

## Slide 17

Anatomy – Analogous Structures – Wings

[ Image: Analogous Structures – Wings ]

Source: National Geographic – Convergent Evolution (nationalgeographic.com)

Description

• Birds and bats both have wings for flying, but their wings are built differently
• Bird wings have feathers; bat wings are made of stretched skin
• They evolved wings separately to solve the same problem: flight

How does this support evolution?

Analogous structures like bird and bat wings show that different animals can independently evolve similar solutions to the same challenge. This process — called convergent evolution — shows how natural selection shapes life in similar ways.

## Slide 18

Biochemistry

Biochemistry compares the chemicals inside living things — like DNA and proteins. The more similar two species' chemistry is, the more closely related they are.

Examples:  DNA Similarity: Humans and Chimps  ·  Cytochrome C Protein  ·  Universal Genetic Code

## Slide 19

Biochemistry – DNA Similarity: Humans and Chimps

[ Image: DNA Similarity: Humans and Chimps ]

Source: Khan Academy – Molecular Evidence for Evolution (khanacademy.org)

Description

• Human DNA and chimpanzee DNA are about 98–99% identical
• The differences in our DNA account for things like brain size and body shape
• The closer two species' DNA, the more recently they shared a common ancestor

How does this support evolution?

The near-identical DNA between humans and chimps is strong evidence that we share a recent common ancestor. Small changes in DNA over millions of years can lead to very different-looking animals.

## Slide 20

Biochemistry – Cytochrome C Protein

[ Image: Cytochrome C Protein ]

Source: Britannica – Cytochrome (britannica.com)

Description

• Cytochrome c is a protein used in energy production found in almost all living things
• The version in humans is nearly identical to that in other mammals
• More distantly related animals (like yeast) have a more different version

How does this support evolution?

Because the same protein is found — with only small changes — across a huge range of life, it shows that all these organisms evolved from a common ancestor a very long time ago. The more changes, the longer ago species split apart.

## Slide 21

Biochemistry – Universal Genetic Code

[ Image: Universal Genetic Code ]

Source: Khan Academy – Universal Genetic Code (khanacademy.org)

Description

• All living things on Earth use the same basic DNA code (A, T, G, C bases)
• The same three-base 'words' (codons) code for the same amino acids in all organisms
• This includes bacteria, plants, fungi, and animals

How does this support evolution?

The fact that all living things share the same genetic language is very strong evidence that life on Earth has one common origin. Evolution built on this original code, leading to the variety of life we see today.
